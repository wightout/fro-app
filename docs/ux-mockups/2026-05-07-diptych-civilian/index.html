The §3 diptych — military-only, or bilingual?

The §3 diptych currently shows a US Army TM page next to a FRO entry. It lands hard for the military audience and reads as authentic-but-foreign for civilian A&Ps, who don't see TMs in their day. Four ways to handle that, ranked by visual cost. Pick one — I'll implement.

Current state

What it does well

  • Authentic to your military background — UH-60M is your home airframe
  • Already implemented and tested
  • Compact section (single diptych, baseline height)

What it costs

  • Civilian A&Ps may read FRO as military-only and bounce
  • "TM 1-1500-204" is foreign jargon to a Cessna mechanic
  • Pills strip says "GA & homebuilders" — but the diptych contradicts that breadth

Option A · Civilian swap

Cessna 172 SMM open full →

What it does well

  • Same section height as current — no layout impact
  • Cessna 172 is the most universal civilian aircraft (≈40% of training fleet)
  • Real document number (D2069-1-13) and real procedure (Camlocs are how Cessna cowlings actually attach)
  • Readable to A&P students, IAs, GA owners, homebuilders

What it costs

  • Loses the military authenticity that matched your background
  • Crew chiefs / military maintainers in the audience strip see a Cessna, not their world
  • Just trades one parochial example for another

Option B · Bilingual triptych

TM + AMM + FRO open full →

What it does well

  • Both audiences see themselves represented — strongest "this is for everyone" read
  • Visualizes the core point: one log, multiple pubs
  • Section grows ~50% (still fits in one band; not stacked)

What it costs

  • Three columns at this width = each panel gets cramped (~33% width vs. 50%)
  • Mobile view collapses to vertical stack anyway, defeating the side-by-side pitch
  • Notes-for-next-time block in FRO entry has to summarize two airframes — denser to read

Option C · Stacked diptychs

Two examples, vertical open full →

What it does well

  • Each panel keeps full readable width — no cramped layout
  • Strongest pedagogical read: same problem, two pubs, twice over
  • Rhymes nicely with the "longitudinal use" framing in §1 (year 1, year 2, year 5)

What it costs

  • Section roughly doubles in height — biggest visual footprint of the four
  • Repetitive content: same procedure, same point, two airframes — risks reader fatigue
  • The diptych lands harder when it's one dense moment, not two
My recommendation

Option A — civilian swap. Reasoning: the audience strip already does the breadth-of-audience work (10 pills covering both worlds). The diptych's job is to make a single "before / after" point land hard. Doing that twice (Option C) or three-up (Option B) dilutes the punch.

A swap to Cessna 172 reads civilian without losing authenticity (Cessna is as iconic in GA as the UH-60M is in Army aviation). The pills strip still claims military, the swap doesn't contradict it — just rebalances the source-of-truth example civilian for one section.

If you'd rather keep the military example and trust the pills strip to do all the heavy lifting, current state is also defensible — it's not broken.
